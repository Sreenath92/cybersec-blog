Why this matters

Most cyber attacks begin with ordinary moments.

A rushed click, a reused password, a fake delivery message, or a public Wi-Fi login can give an attacker the opening they need. The goal of this blog is not to scare people. It is to make safer choices feel normal, simple, and repeatable.

Common hacking types

Know the tricks before they reach you.

01

Phishing

Fake emails, texts, or websites pressure you to reveal passwords, payment details, or one-time codes.

Protect yourself:

Open websites from bookmarks, check sender addresses, and never share verification codes.

02

Password Attacks

Attackers try leaked, weak, or reused passwords to break into email, banking, and social accounts.

Protect yourself:

Use a password manager, unique passwords, and multi-factor authentication.

03

Malware and Ransomware

Harmful apps or files can steal data, spy on activity, or lock files until money is demanded.

Protect yourself:

Install updates, avoid unknown downloads, and keep offline or cloud backups.

04

Social Engineering

Scammers pretend to be support staff, family, employers, or officials to rush you into action.

Protect yourself:

Pause, verify through a trusted channel, and do not act on urgency alone.

05

Public Wi-Fi Snooping

Unsafe networks can expose browsing activity or trick you into joining fake hotspots.

Protect yourself:

Use mobile data or a trusted VPN for sensitive activity, and avoid unknown hotspots.

06

SIM Swapping

Criminals try to move your phone number to their SIM card so they can receive account codes.

Protect yourself:

Add a carrier PIN and use app-based authentication instead of SMS when possible.

Personal protection checklist

Small habits that block big problems.

You do not need to be technical to be safer online. Start with these habits and build from there.

If something goes wrong

Act quickly, calmly, and in the right order.

  1. Disconnect: Turn off Wi-Fi or unplug the network if a device looks infected.
  2. Change passwords: Start with email, banking, and social accounts from a safe device.
  3. Enable MFA: Add app-based authentication where available.
  4. Contact providers: Tell your bank, carrier, school, or workplace if accounts are affected.
  5. Report: In the U.S., report fraud at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

CyberSafe Basics is an awareness resource. For serious incidents, contact your bank, service provider, or local cybercrime authority.
